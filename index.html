Fluid Mechanics · Chapter 1

Fluid Properties

What makes a fluid a fluid — and the handful of properties (density, viscosity, surface tension, compressibility) that govern everything that follows.

DENSITYVISCOSITYSURFACE TENSIONCAPILLARITY

TheoryThe language of fluids

Learning objectives

  • Define a fluid and distinguish it from a solid through its response to shear.
  • Use density, specific weight and specific gravity interchangeably and correctly.
  • Apply Newton's law of viscosity to compute shear stress and viscous forces.
  • Estimate capillary rise and explain the role of surface tension and vapor pressure.

1.1 What is a fluid?

A fluid is a substance that deforms continuously under the action of a shear stress, no matter how small that stress is. A solid resists shear with a finite deformation; a fluid simply keeps flowing. Liquids and gases are both fluids — liquids take the shape of their container and form a free surface, while gases expand to fill it completely. In this course we treat the fluid as a continuum: properties such as density are smooth functions of position, because even a cubic micrometre of water contains ~3×10¹⁰ molecules.

1.2 Density, specific weight, specific gravity

ρ = mV [kg/m³],   γ = ρg [N/m³],   SG = ρρwater

For water at 20 °C, ρ ≈ 998 kg/m³ and γ ≈ 9.79 kN/m³ (the round value γ = 9.81 kN/m³ for ρ = 1000 kg/m³ is standard in hand calculations). Mercury has SG = 13.6 — the reason it makes such compact manometers.

1.3 Viscosity — Newton's law

Viscosity measures a fluid's resistance to shearing motion: it is the internal friction between fluid layers. For the linear velocity profile between a fixed and a moving plate, the shear stress is proportional to the velocity gradient:

τ = μ dudyτ — shear stress [Pa] · μ — dynamic viscosity [Pa·s] · du/dy — velocity gradient [s⁻¹]

Dynamic viscosity μ [Pa·s] and kinematic viscosity ν = μ/ρ [m²/s] both appear constantly; ν is what enters the Reynolds number. Fluids obeying τ = μ·du/dy with constant μ are Newtonian (water, air, oils). Non-Newtonian fluids deviate: shear-thinning fluids like drilling mud and paint flow more easily as they are sheared, shear-thickening suspensions stiffen, and Bingham plastics (toothpaste, fresh concrete) need a finite yield stress before they flow at all — a behaviour central to pumping concrete and to drilling-fluid hydraulics.

Watch the temperature. Heating a liquid lowers its viscosity (molecular cohesion weakens); heating a gas raises it (molecular collisions intensify). Always take μ and ν at the working temperature.

1.4 Compressibility and vapor pressure

The bulk modulus Ev = −dp/(dV/V) measures resistance to compression. For water Ev ≈ 2.2 GPa: raising the pressure by 1 MPa squeezes its volume by only ~0.05%, which is why liquid flows are treated as incompressible (and why sudden valve closure produces violent water hammer). The vapor pressure pv is the pressure at which a liquid boils at the local temperature. If the absolute pressure anywhere in a flow drops to pv, vapor bubbles form and collapse — cavitation — eroding pump impellers and turbine blades. Keeping p > pv is a real design constraint you will meet again in Chapters 4 and 13.

1.5 Surface tension and capillarity

Molecules at a liquid surface are pulled inward, making the surface behave like a stretched membrane with tension σ [N/m]. In a small tube of diameter d, this membrane drags the liquid up (wetting, θ < 90°, e.g. water on glass) or pushes it down (non-wetting, e.g. mercury):

h = 4σ cos θγ dcapillary rise · θ — contact angle (≈ 0° for water on clean glass)

Capillarity matters in soils, in porous building materials and — practically for us — as a reading error in small manometer tubes: use tubes larger than about 10 mm to keep it negligible.

Fluid Statics

Pressure in a fluid at rest, manometers, hydrostatic forces on gates and dams, and why ships float — the oldest and most exam-relevant part of the course.

p = γhMANOMETRYFORCES ON SURFACESBUOYANCY

TheoryPressure at rest

Learning objectives

  • Compute pressure variation with depth and convert between absolute and gage pressure.
  • Read any manometer by walking through it limb by limb.
  • Find the magnitude and line of action of the hydrostatic force on plane surfaces.
  • Apply Archimedes' principle to floating and submerged bodies.

2.1 Pressure and its variation with depth

Pascal's law: at a point in a fluid at rest, pressure acts equally in all directions. In the vertical, force balance on a fluid element gives dp/dz = −γ, so in a liquid of constant density the pressure grows linearly with depth h below the free surface:

p = p0 + γh  ⇒  pgage = γhevery 10 m of water adds ≈ 98.1 kPa ≈ 1 atm

Absolute pressure is measured from vacuum; gage pressure from local atmospheric: pabs = patm + pgage. Civil-engineering hydraulics works almost entirely in gage pressure — but cavitation checks (Ch. 1) must use absolute values. Note that pressure depends only on depth, not on container shape: connected points at the same level in the same static fluid share the same pressure — the principle behind every manometer and every hydraulic jack.

2.2 Manometry

A manometer converts a pressure difference into a column height. The reading rule: start at a point of known pressure and add γ·Δz going down, subtract going up, until you reach the target point.

2.3 Hydrostatic force on a plane surface

Pressure grows with depth, so a submerged gate feels a distributed, triangular-to-trapezoidal load. Integrating it gives two clean results for any plane surface of area A whose centroid lies at depth hc:

FR = γ hc A,    yp = yc + Ixcyc Ay measured along the surface from the free-surface line · Ixc = b a³/12 for a rectangle a tall, b wide

The resultant acts at the center of pressure, always below the centroid (the deeper half of the surface carries more load). For a rectangle whose top edge lies at the free surface, the classical results follow: F = γH²b/2 acting at H/3 above the base — try it live below.

2.4 Curved surfaces — in one breath

For a curved surface (radial gates, tank shells): the horizontal force component equals the force on the vertical projection of the surface, and the vertical component equals the weight of the liquid volume above the surface (real or imaginary). The two components are then combined vectorially.

2.5 Buoyancy and floating bodies

FB = γfluid VdisplacedArchimedes: the net upward pressure force equals the weight of displaced fluid, acting at the centroid of the displaced volume

A body floats when it can displace fluid weighing as much as itself, so a floating body of specific gravity SG sits with a fraction SG of its volume submerged (in fresh water). Stability of ships adds one more idea: the metacentre; the vessel is stable when GM = I₀/Vdisp − BG > 0, i.e. a wide waterline (large I₀) fights capsizing.

Kinematics & Continuity

Describing motion before explaining it: velocity fields, streamlines, how we classify flows — and the conservation of mass that every later chapter silently relies on.

STREAMLINESFLOW CLASSIFICATIONQ = AVCONTINUITY

TheoryDescribing fluid motion

Learning objectives

  • Distinguish the Lagrangian and Eulerian descriptions and the flow lines used to visualise them.
  • Classify a flow as steady/unsteady, uniform/non-uniform, and 1-D/2-D/3-D.
  • Compute volumetric and mass flow rates and apply the continuity equation.

3.1 Two ways to watch a flow

The Lagrangian description follows individual fluid particles (tag a droplet, track its path). The Eulerian description — the one fluid mechanics actually uses — fixes attention on points in space and records the velocity field V(x, y, z, t) passing through them. Three families of lines visualise this field: a streamline is everywhere tangent to the instantaneous velocity; a pathline is the trajectory of one particle; a streakline joins all particles that passed through one point (a dye trace). In steady flow the three coincide — one reason steady flow is so much easier to analyse.

A particle in an Eulerian field accelerates for two reasons: the field itself changes in time (local acceleration, ∂V/∂t) and the particle moves to a place where velocity differs (convective acceleration). Even in steady flow, convective acceleration is alive and well — water speeds up through a nozzle although nothing changes in time.

Pipe and channel hydraulics thrives on the one-dimensional idealisation: we work with the cross-section-averaged velocity V = Q/A and let correction factors absorb the profile shape when needed.

3.3 Flow rate and the continuity equation

Q = AV [m³/s],   ṁ = ρAV [kg/s]

Mass is conserved: for steady flow through a streamtube, whatever mass enters must leave. Between any two sections,

ρ₁A₁V₁ = ρ₂A₂V₂   →   A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ the right form holds for incompressible flow — the workhorse of this course
Rule of thumb. Halve the pipe diameter and the area drops 4× — so the velocity jumps 4× and (Ch. 7) the friction loss per metre roughly 16×. Diameter choices dominate hydraulic design.

Key takeaways. Streamlines map the instantaneous field · steady ≠ no acceleration (convective terms survive) · Q = AV and A₁V₁ = A₂V₂ are used in nearly every problem from here on.

Bernoulli & the Energy Equation

The most famous equation in fluid mechanics: pressure, velocity and elevation trading places along a streamline — and its grown-up version with pumps, turbines and losses.

HEADSEGL / HGLPITOTVENTURI

TheoryEnergy along a streamline

Learning objectives

  • State the Bernoulli equation, its three head terms and its assumptions.
  • Sketch and interpret the energy and hydraulic grade lines (EGL/HGL).
  • Apply Bernoulli to Pitot tubes, Venturi meters, orifices and free jets.
  • Extend the equation with pump head, turbine head and losses.

4.1 The Bernoulli equation

Integrating Newton's second law along a streamline for steady, incompressible, frictionless flow gives a statement of mechanical-energy conservation. Written per unit weight, every term is a length — a head:

pγ + V²2g + z = H = const along a streamlinepressure head + velocity head + elevation head = total head

The Hydraulic Grade Line (HGL) plots p/γ + z — the level water would reach in a piezometer tap. The Energy Grade Line (EGL) sits one velocity head V²/2g above it. In ideal flow the EGL is horizontal; real friction (Ch. 7) tilts it steadily downhill.

Check the assumptions before using Bernoulli: steady · incompressible · negligible friction · along a streamline · no pump/turbine between the points. Break one, and you need the extended energy equation of §4.4.

4.2 Static, dynamic and stagnation pressure

Bringing a stream to rest converts its kinetic energy into pressure: the stagnation pressure p₀ = p + ½ρV². A Pitot-static tube measures both p₀ and p, and the difference reveals the velocity:

V = √2(p₀ − p)/ρ = √2gΔhΔh — manometer reading in the flowing liquid's own column

4.3 Classic applications

Torricelli's jet. Between a large tank's surface and a small orifice at depth h (both at atmospheric pressure, surface velocity ≈ 0), Bernoulli collapses to V = √(2gh) — a free jet leaves with the speed of free fall from the surface.

Venturi meter. Squeezing the flow through a throat raises V and drops p; measuring Δp between inlet and throat, with continuity, yields the flow rate (Example 4.1). The same physics feeds carburettors, injectors and cavitation checks. Watch it live:

Interactive · Venturi tube — trade pressure for speed

Particles accelerate through the throat while the piezometer columns (the HGL) dip. D₁ = 150 mm, throat D₂ = 75 mm, so the throat runs 4× faster.
V₂ = 4.8 m/s p₁ − p₂ = 10.8 kPa
Δp = ρ(V₂² − V₁²)/2 with V₂ = 4V₁. Faster where narrower, lower pressure where faster — Bernoulli in one picture.

4.4 The extended energy equation

Real systems add machines and lose head to friction. Per unit weight, between sections 1 and 2:

p₁γ+V₁²2g+z₁+hp = p₂γ+V₂²2g+z₂+ht+hLhp — head added by a pump · ht — head extracted by a turbine · hL — head lost to friction (Ch. 7–8)

This single line is the backbone of pipeline design; Chapters 7, 8 and 13 are devoted to evaluating hL and hp.

The Momentum Equation

When you need a force — on a nozzle, a pipe bend, a spillway deflector — energy methods stay silent. Newton's second law, written for a control volume, speaks.

CONTROL VOLUMEJETSPIPE BENDSANCHOR FORCES

TheoryForces from changing momentum

Learning objectives

  • Apply the linear momentum equation to a fixed control volume.
  • Compute jet forces on plates and vanes.
  • Find anchor forces on bends and nozzles, including the pressure contributions.

5.1 The linear momentum equation

Newton's second law for a fixed control volume in steady flow with one inlet (1) and one outlet (2) reads, as a vector equation:

ΣF⃗ = ṁ(V⃗₂ − V⃗₁) = ρQ(V⃗₂ − V⃗₁)ΣF⃗ — all external forces on the fluid in the CV: pressure forces on the cut sections, weight, and the reaction R⃗ from solid boundaries

Three habits prevent nearly all mistakes: (1) draw the control volume and cut it where you know the pressures; (2) use gage pressures so the surrounding atmosphere cancels out; (3) keep it a vector equation — solve x and y components separately, then combine. The force the fluid exerts on the structure is the reaction −R⃗.

5.2 Jet striking a plate

A horizontal jet hitting a fixed vertical plate leaves radially: its x-momentum is destroyed, so the plate pushes on the fluid with F = ρQ(0 − V), i.e. the fluid pushes the plate with F = ρQV = ρAV² — the working principle of Pelton buckets (which, by turning the jet through 180°, double the punch to 2ρQV).

5.3 Bends and nozzles

In a pressurised bend the pressure forces on the cut sections usually dwarf the pure momentum-change term — never forget them. Example 5.2 walks through the full recipe: continuity → Bernoulli for the unknown pressure → momentum in x and y → resultant anchor force. That anchor force is what thrust blocks on water mains are designed to resist.

6.1 Dimensions and the Buckingham Pi theorem

Every fluid variable is built from three primary dimensions: mass M, length L, time T (e.g. pressure = ML⁻¹T⁻²; viscosity μ = ML⁻¹T⁻¹). Choosing r repeating variables (typically a density, a velocity, a length) and folding each remaining variable in, an experiment over five variables collapses to a single curve of Π₁ versus Π₂ — this is precisely how the Moody chart of Chapter 7 was born.

8.2 Pipes in series and in parallel

Series: the same Q passes every pipe; total loss = ΣhL,i.
Parallel: every branch spans the same two nodes, so hL,1 = hL,2 = … ; total flow = ΣQi. The flow splits itself so the head losses agree — exactly like currents in parallel resistors.

8.3 Branches and networks

The classical three-reservoir problem (three pipes meeting at one junction) is solved by guessing the junction head, computing the three flows, and adjusting until continuity at the junction balances. City-scale looped networks generalise this: the Hardy Cross method iteratively corrects an assumed flow distribution so that ΣQ = 0 at every node and ΣhL = 0 around every loop — the hand-calculation ancestor of today's network software (e.g. EPANET).

10.1 Specific energy

Specific energy is the energy head measured from the channel bed. For discharge per unit width q = Q/b in a rectangular channel:

E = y + V²2g = y + q²2gy²

Plotted against depth for fixed q, E has a striking shape: large at very small depths (fast, shallow flow), large again at big depths (E → y), with a single minimum in between. For any E above that minimum the same discharge can flow at two alternate depths — one deep and tranquil, one shallow and shooting.

10.2 Critical flow

Setting dE/dy = 0 gives the general critical condition and, for rectangular channels, closed forms:

Q²TgA³ = 1   ⇒ (rectangular)   yc = (q²g)1/3,  Vc = √g yc,  Emin = 32 yc

Critical velocity equals the speed of a small gravity wave, √(gy) — which is exactly what the Froude number compares:

10.3 Transitions

A smooth hump on the bed or a gentle width contraction "spends" specific energy: subcritical flow responds by dropping its surface over the hump, supercritical flow by rising — opposite behaviours from the two limbs of the same curve. Raise the hump until E would fall below Emin and the flow chokes: it must back up and pass through critical right on the crest, which is precisely how broad-crested weirs (Ch. 12) measure discharge.

11.1 Why the flow jumps

Supercritical flow (from a sluice gate, a chute, a spillway toe) meets a downstream reach that insists on subcritical depth. The flow cannot glide smoothly through critical while gaining the energy that path would require — so it leaps: a turbulent roller forms, air entrains, and a large share of the kinetic energy is destroyed as heat. Because the loss is unknown in advance, the energy equation is useless across the jump; the momentum equation, indifferent to internal dissipation, is what connects the two sides.

11.2 The Bélanger equation

Equating momentum + pressure force per unit width across a jump in a horizontal rectangular channel gives the sequent-depth ratio:

The energy destroyed then follows from the depths alone — a remarkably compact result:

ΔE = (1.95−0.4)³4×0.4×1.95 = 1.20 m,   P = γqΔE = 9810×3×1.20 ≈ 35.2 kW/m
Answer. An oscillating jump destroying ≈ 35 kW per metre of width (≈ 37% of the incoming energy). In a 20 m-wide basin that is 0.7 MW turned harmlessly into heat and spray.
Key takeaways. Jumps connect supercritical to subcritical flow · analyse with momentum, never energy · y₂/y₁ = ½(√(1+8Fr₁²)−1) · ΔE = (y₂−y₁)³/4y₁y₂ · stilling basins are engineered jumps.

Orifices, Venturi meters and weirs: how a depth reading or a pressure tap becomes a trustworthy discharge — the metrology of water.

TheoryFrom reading to discharge

Learning objectives

  • Apply orifice and Venturi equations with the proper discharge coefficients.
  • Compute discharge over sharp-crested rectangular and triangular weirs.
  • Choose the right device for the flow range and accuracy required.

12.1 Orifices and coefficients

Torricelli (Ch. 4) gives the ideal jet velocity √(2gh); reality trims it twice. The jet contracts to a vena contracta (coefficient Cc ≈ 0.62 for a sharp edge) and loses a little speed to friction (Cv ≈ 0.97). Their product is the discharge coefficient:

Q = Cd A √2gh,   Cd = CcCv ≈ 0.60A — orifice area · h — head above the orifice centre

12.2 Pressure-differential meters

The Venturi meter converts Example 4.1 into an instrument: with a measured head difference Δh between inlet (1) and throat (2),

Q = Cd A₂ √2gΔh1 − (A₂/A₁)²Cd ≈ 0.96–0.99 — the diffuser recovers most of the pressure, so Venturis waste very little head

Orifice plates use the same formula with Cd ≈ 0.6: far cheaper, far more head-hungry. Nozzles sit between the two.

12.3 Weirs — measuring open-channel flow

A sharp-crested weir is a thin plate over which the stream springs clear (the nappe). Integrating the ideal velocity distribution over the head H and correcting with Cd:

Q = 23Cd√2g · b · H3/2 rectangular, Cd ≈ 0.62    Q = 815Cd√2g tanθ2 H5/2 V-notch, Cd ≈ 0.58

The exponents tell you which tool to pick: the V-notch's H5/2 keeps small discharges readable (laboratory flumes, small streams), while the rectangular weir's width b gives capacity for larger channels. The broad-crested weir instead forces the flow through critical depth on its crest, giving Q ≈ 1.7·b·H3/2 (SI, ideal) directly from Chapter 10's theory — robust, and kind to floating debris.

Good practice. Measure H well upstream of the drawdown (≥ 4H), keep the nappe ventilated, and respect each formula's calibration range — a weir is only as good as its head reading.

Pumps, Turbines & Systems

Machines meet pipelines: how a pump curve and a system curve negotiate an operating point, what cavitation forbids, and how turbines run the story in reverse.

PUMP HEADSYSTEM CURVEOPERATING POINTNPSH

TheoryAdding and extracting head

Learning objectives

  • Insert pump and turbine heads into the energy equation and compute power.
  • Construct the system curve and locate the operating point on a pump curve.
  • State the cavitation (NPSH) requirement and the basic turbine families.

13.1 Pump head and power

A pump adds head hp between its flanges (Ch. 4's extended energy equation). The useful hydraulic power, and the shaft power once efficiency is paid, are:

Phydraulic = γQhp,    Pshaft = γQhpηpumpchain the efficiencies: Pelectrical = γQhp/(ηpumpηmotor)

13.2 System curve meets pump curve

The pipeline demands head that grows with flow — static lift plus friction:

Hsystem = Δz + (fLD + ΣK)V²2g = Δz + kQ²

The pump offers head that falls with flow (its characteristic curve, roughly H = H₀ − aQ²). Where the two curves cross is the operating point — the one (Q, H) pair at which the installed system can run:

Pump curve × system curve — the operating point

Drawn live: pump H = 40 − 600Q² against system H = 10 + 300Q². They intersect at Q ≈ 0.183 m³/s, H ≈ 20 m.
Throttling a valve steepens the system curve (point slides up-left); trimming the impeller or slowing the motor lowers the pump curve. Two identical pumps in parallel double Q at each H; in series they double H at each Q.

13.3 Cavitation and NPSH

The lowest pressure in the circuit lives at the pump's impeller eye. If it reaches the vapor pressure, bubbles form and collapse against the blades — cavitation: noise, vibration, pitting, dying performance. The margin is expressed as Net Positive Suction Head: the installation must supply more than the pump demands, NPSHavailable > NPSHrequired. Practical levers: mount the pump low, keep the suction pipe short and generous, mind hot liquids (high pv).

13.4 Turbines in one paragraph

A turbine extracts ht and delivers P = γQhtηt. Impulse machines (Pelton) convert all head to jet velocity and suit high-head, low-flow sites — they are Chapter 5's jet-on-a-vane, industrialised. Reaction machines (Francis, Kaplan) run full of pressurised water and cover medium and low heads; Kaplan's adjustable blades keep efficiency high as rivers rise and fall. The dimensionless specific speed (Ch. 6 thinking) tells you which family fits a given Q–H duty.

Key takeaways. P = γQH, divided by η for pumps and multiplied for turbines · the system runs where pump and system curves intersect · guard NPSH against cavitation · Pelton for high head, Francis/Kaplan as head falls.
